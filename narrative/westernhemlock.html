Fun Fact

Western Hemlock

According to Pacific Northwest Indigenous tradition, when Mother Nature gave out branches and needles to young trees, the Hemlock arrived last. By the time it reached the front, all the grand branches were gone. All the long needles had been claimed.

What remained were small, soft needles no other tree wanted. Short branches that drooped instead of reaching skyward. Just enough to survive, but nothing to boast about. The hemlock took what was left without complaint.

With those humble gifts, it became one of the most abundant trees in the forest, feeding the land, sheltering creatures, and standing strong through centuries.

Humble beginnings can lead to the strongest roots.

Trees

Western Hemlock

The Western Hemlock thrives in the cool, misty forests of the Pacific Northwest, stretching from Alaska to Northern California. It is tall and graceful producing cones and can grow to 150 to 200 feet tall.

The Washington state tree is the Western Hemlock reflecting Washington's blue-collar innovators who changed the world from a rainy corner of the country, building cities from forests.
